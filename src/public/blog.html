Why UK Supply Chains Are Still Broken in 2025 — And How to Fix Them

Despite billions invested in digital transformation, the majority of UK supply chains remain fragmented, reactive, and dangerously exposed to disruption. From NHS procurement backlogs to Tier-1 construction delays, the root cause is almost always the same: siloed data, manual processes, and a lack of real-time visibility.

The Scale of the Problem

A 2024 report by the Chartered Institute of Procurement & Supply (CIPS) found that 74% of UK businesses experienced at least one significant supply chain disruption in the past 12 months. For NHS trusts, the figure is even higher — with procurement inefficiencies costing the health service an estimated £1.3 billion annually in avoidable waste and emergency sourcing premiums.

In construction, the picture is equally stark. Material delivery delays are now cited as the number one cause of project overruns, with Tier-1 contractors reporting that subcontractor misalignment adds an average of 11% to final project costs. These aren't abstract statistics — they represent missed deadlines, liquidated damages, and reputational damage that compounds over time.

The Five Root Causes of Supply Chain Failure

  • 01.
    Fragmented procurement data. When purchasing decisions are made across multiple departments with no central visibility, duplicate vendor spend and missed volume discounts are inevitable.
  • 02.
    Legacy ERP systems that don't communicate. Many organisations are running 15–20 year old software that cannot integrate with modern logistics platforms, creating dangerous data blind spots.
  • 03.
    Reactive contractor management. Without automated compliance tracking, organisations only discover a contractor's certification has lapsed after an incident — not before.
  • 04.
    No single source of truth. When project managers, procurement teams, and the boardroom are all working from different data sets, strategic decisions are made on outdated information.
  • 05.
    Over-reliance on email and spreadsheets. Critical supply chain decisions are still being made via email chains that get lost, misread, or simply never actioned.

What a Fixed Supply Chain Actually Looks Like

The organisations that have successfully modernised their supply chains share three common characteristics. First, they have centralised their procurement data into a single platform — eliminating the departmental silos that create duplicate spend. Second, they have automated contractor compliance, so certifications, insurance documents, and safety records are verified in real time rather than manually checked. Third, they have invested in predictive visibility tools that flag risks 48–72 hours before they reach the critical path.

At Troer, we have spent 15 years implementing exactly this kind of transformation — not as theoretical consultants, but as hands-on project managers who embed within your team and execute the change. Our TroerConnect platform bridges legacy ERP systems with modern cloud environments, while TroerTrack provides the predictive risk intelligence that turns reactive firefighting into proactive resilience.

The Cost of Doing Nothing

The most dangerous position any organisation can take is to acknowledge the problem and delay the fix. Every month of inaction compounds the inefficiency. Duplicate vendor spend continues. Contractor risks go undetected. Project timelines slip. And the gap between your operation and your competitors who have already modernised grows wider. The question is no longer whether to fix your supply chain — it's how quickly you can afford to start.

NHS Supply Chain Reform: Why Centralised Procurement Is the Only Way Forward

The NHS spends over £27 billion annually on goods and services. Yet despite this enormous purchasing power, fragmented procurement across hundreds of trusts means the health service routinely pays more than it should, receives less than it needs, and responds to supply failures far too slowly. Centralised procurement is not a luxury — it is an operational necessity.

The Hidden Cost of Fragmented NHS Procurement

When individual NHS trusts procure independently, they lose the collective bargaining power that comes with consolidated spend. A single trust ordering 500 units of a medical consumable will always pay more per unit than a centralised system ordering 50,000. Multiply this across thousands of product lines and hundreds of trusts, and the waste becomes staggering. NHS England's own data suggests that better procurement coordination could save the health service up to £1.3 billion per year — funds that could be redirected directly into frontline care.

The Three Pillars of Effective NHS Procurement Reform

  • 01.
    Consolidated vendor management. Reducing the number of approved suppliers and negotiating framework agreements that deliver consistent pricing, quality standards, and delivery reliability across all trusts.
  • 02.
    Real-time inventory visibility. Implementing systems that give procurement teams live sight of stock levels across all sites, eliminating both costly overstocking and dangerous shortfalls.
  • 03.
    Automated compliance and audit trails. Ensuring every supplier meets NHS clinical standards, with certifications verified automatically rather than through manual spot-checks that inevitably miss gaps.

What Centralisation Looks Like in Practice

Effective NHS procurement centralisation doesn't mean removing local autonomy entirely. It means creating a shared infrastructure — a single source of truth for supplier data, pricing, and compliance — that individual trusts can draw from while still managing their specific operational requirements. The technology to do this exists. The challenge is implementation: migrating legacy systems, retraining procurement teams, and managing the change process without disrupting clinical operations.

We have managed procurement centralisation projects across multiple NHS trusts, navigating the political, operational, and technical complexities that derail less experienced teams. The Urgency Has Never Been Greater

With NHS budgets under unprecedented pressure and clinical demand continuing to rise, the margin for procurement inefficiency has effectively disappeared. Every pound wasted on fragmented purchasing is a pound not spent on patient care. The trusts that move fastest on centralisation will be best positioned to weather the financial pressures ahead — and to deliver the standard of care their patients deserve.

Tier-1 Contractor Management: The 7 Mistakes That Blow Construction Budgets

Construction projects in the UK are failing at an alarming rate. According to KPMG's Global Construction Survey, only 31% of projects come within 10% of their original budget. The primary culprit in the majority of cases isn't material costs or design changes — it's poor Tier-1 contractor management. Here are the seven mistakes that consistently derail projects and how to eliminate them.

Mistake 1: Onboarding Contractors Without Verified Compliance

Allowing contractors onto site before their certifications, insurance documents, and safety records have been independently verified is one of the most common and costly mistakes in construction project management. A single unverified contractor can trigger HSE investigations, insurance voids, and project shutdowns that cost far more than the compliance check would have.

Mistake 2: No Single Source of Truth for Subcontractor Data

When contractor performance data, delivery schedules, and compliance records are spread across emails, spreadsheets, and individual project managers' notebooks, critical information gets lost. A centralised contractor management system that gives every stakeholder real-time access to the same data is not optional on a complex infrastructure project — it is essential.

Mistake 3: Reactive Rather Than Predictive Scheduling

Most construction project managers only discover a scheduling conflict when it has already caused a delay. Predictive scheduling tools that model dependencies, flag resource conflicts, and identify critical path risks 48-72 hours in advance give project teams the window they need to pivot before a delay becomes a cost overrun.

Mistake 4: Ignoring JIT Delivery Alignment

Just-in-Time material delivery is one of the most powerful cost-control tools available to construction project managers — but only when it is properly coordinated. Materials arriving too early create storage costs and theft risk. Materials arriving late halt work entirely. Precise JIT alignment requires real-time communication between procurement, logistics, and site management that most projects simply don't have.

Mistake 5: Underestimating Tier-2 and Tier-3 Risk

Tier-1 contractors are typically well-managed. The risk lives further down the supply chain. A Tier-3 specialist subcontractor failing to deliver a critical component can cascade upwards and halt an entire project. Effective contractor management requires visibility not just of your direct suppliers but of their suppliers too.

Mistake 6: No KPI Framework for Contractor Performance

Without clearly defined, measurable KPIs — on-time delivery rate, defect rate, safety incident frequency, response time to instructions — contractor performance becomes subjective. Subjective assessments lead to disputes, delayed payments, and ultimately litigation. A robust KPI framework protects both parties and drives continuous improvement.

Mistake 7: Treating the Post-Project Review as Optional

The organisations that consistently deliver projects on time and on budget are the ones that conduct rigorous post-project reviews and feed the lessons learned back into their contractor management processes. Without this feedback loop, the same mistakes repeat on every project. With it, each project becomes more efficient than the last.

Predictive Risk Management: How to See Supply Chain Disruptions Before They Happen

The era of reactive supply chain management is over. Businesses that wait for disruptions to materialise before responding are operating with a fundamental competitive disadvantage. The organisations pulling ahead in 2025 are those that have shifted from reactive firefighting to predictive resilience — using data to identify risks 48 to 72 hours before they reach the critical path.

Why Reactive Management Is No Longer Viable

In a supply chain with dozens of interdependent variables — freight partners, Tier-1 and Tier-2 suppliers, port clearance windows, weather events, and regulatory changes — a single unexpected disruption can cascade into a project-wide crisis within hours. By the time a reactive team has identified the problem, escalated it, and begun sourcing alternatives, the damage is already done. Liquidated damages are accruing. Clinical operations are impacted. Client relationships are strained.

The Four Data Sources That Predict Disruption

  • 01.
    Supplier performance history. A supplier who has missed three of their last ten delivery windows is statistically likely to miss the next one. Historical performance data is the most reliable predictor of future behaviour.
  • 02.
    Port and freight congestion data. Real-time monitoring of port congestion levels, freight capacity constraints, and customs clearance delays allows logistics teams to reroute shipments before delays become critical.
  • 03.
    Weather and geopolitical risk feeds. Severe weather events and geopolitical instability are increasingly frequent supply chain disruptors. Integrating external risk feeds into your supply chain monitoring gives early warning of events that could impact your logistics network.
  • 04.
    Internal milestone tracking. When project milestones are tracked in real time against planned schedules, slippage becomes visible the moment it begins — not weeks later when it has compounded into a crisis.

Building a Predictive Risk Framework

Effective predictive risk management requires three things working in concert: the right data sources feeding into a centralised monitoring platform, automated alert thresholds that trigger notifications when risk indicators exceed acceptable levels, and experienced project managers who know how to interpret the signals and act decisively. Technology without human expertise produces noise. Human expertise without technology produces blind spots. The combination produces resilience.

In industries where margins are tight and client expectations are high, the ability to consistently deliver on time is a genuine competitive differentiator. Businesses that can demonstrate a track record of proactive risk management win more contracts, retain more clients, and build the kind of reputation that generates referrals. How to Cut Procurement Costs by 25% Without Cutting Corners

Every procurement director has been told to cut costs. The instinctive response — squeezing suppliers on price, reducing order quantities, or switching to cheaper alternatives — almost always backfires. Quality drops. Relationships deteriorate. Hidden costs emerge elsewhere in the supply chain. The organisations that achieve sustainable procurement savings do it differently: they eliminate waste rather than value.

Where the Real Waste Lives

In our 15 years of procurement audits across NHS trusts, construction firms, and enterprise businesses, the same categories of waste appear repeatedly. Duplicate vendor spend — where multiple departments are buying the same product from different suppliers at different prices — typically accounts for 8 to 12% of total procurement spend. Maverick purchasing — where individuals bypass approved procurement channels — adds another 5 to 8%. Emergency sourcing premiums, paid when poor planning forces last-minute purchases, can add 15 to 30% to the cost of individual orders.

The Five Levers of Sustainable Cost Reduction

  • 01.
    Vendor consolidation. Reducing your approved supplier list and concentrating spend with fewer, better-performing vendors unlocks volume discounts and reduces the administrative overhead of managing multiple relationships.
  • 02.
    Spend visibility. You cannot manage what you cannot see. A centralised procurement platform that gives real-time visibility of all purchasing activity across all departments is the foundation of any cost reduction programme.
  • 03.
    Contract compliance monitoring. Many organisations negotiate excellent framework agreements and then fail to enforce them. Automated contract compliance monitoring ensures that agreed pricing and terms are actually applied to every purchase order.
  • 04.
    Demand forecasting. Accurate demand forecasting eliminates both the cost of overstocking and the premium of emergency purchasing. Integrating procurement data with operational planning systems is the key to getting this right.
  • 05.
    Invoice audit and recovery. Systematic auditing of supplier invoices against agreed contract terms consistently uncovers overcharges, duplicate billings, and unapplied credits. In high-volume procurement environments, this alone can recover significant sums.

The 25% Target Is Achievable

A 25% reduction in procurement costs sounds ambitious. In our experience, it is consistently achievable for organisations that have not previously undertaken a structured procurement optimisation programme. The savings are not found in one place — they are accumulated across vendor consolidation, spend visibility, contract compliance, and waste elimination. Each lever delivers a modest improvement. Together, they compound into transformational savings.

The critical success factor is not the technology — it is the implementation. Procurement optimisation programmes fail when they are treated as IT projects rather than organisational change programmes. The technology enables the saving. The change management delivers it. This is why Troer's approach combines best-in-class procurement platforms with 15 years of hands-on change management experience — because one without the other simply doesn't work.

Just-In-Time Delivery in 2025: Why JIT Is Back and How to Make It Work

After years of being written off in the wake of pandemic-era supply chain chaos, Just-In-Time delivery is making a powerful comeback. Businesses that abandoned JIT in favour of safety stock buffers are now sitting on expensive excess inventory while their more agile competitors are running leaner, faster, and more profitably. The lesson of the last five years isn't that JIT doesn't work — it's that JIT without real-time visibility doesn't work.

What Went Wrong With JIT During the Pandemic

The supply chain disruptions of 2020 to 2022 exposed a fundamental weakness in how most organisations had implemented JIT: they had optimised for efficiency without building in resilience. Single-source supplier relationships, minimal buffer stock, and no real-time visibility of upstream supply chain risks meant that when disruption hit, there was no early warning and no fallback. The problem wasn't JIT itself — it was JIT implemented without the data infrastructure to support it.

The New JIT: Precision Delivery Backed by Real-Time Intelligence

The organisations successfully running JIT in 2025 have rebuilt their approach around three principles. First, multi-source supplier relationships that provide alternative supply routes when primary sources are disrupted. Second, real-time visibility of the entire supply chain — from raw material availability at Tier-2 suppliers through to last-mile delivery — so that potential disruptions are identified and rerouted before they cause delays. Third, dynamic scheduling systems that can adjust delivery windows in real time based on live operational data.

JIT in Construction: The Highest-Stakes Application

In construction, JIT delivery is not a cost optimisation strategy — it is an operational necessity. Construction sites have limited storage capacity, and materials arriving too early create theft risk, damage risk, and site congestion that slows all other work. Materials arriving late halt work entirely, triggering liquidated damages that can run to tens of thousands of pounds per day on major infrastructure projects. Precise JIT coordination in construction requires real-time communication between procurement, logistics partners, and site management that most projects still lack.

JIT in NHS Logistics: Where Precision Saves Lives

In healthcare, JIT takes on an even greater significance. Clinical teams need the right consumables, pharmaceuticals, and equipment at the right time — not days early creating storage problems, and certainly not late creating clinical risk. NHS trusts that have implemented properly supported JIT logistics report significant reductions in both storage costs and emergency procurement premiums, while improving the reliability of supply to frontline teams.

Making JIT Work: The Non-Negotiables

  • 01.
    Real-time supplier visibility. You must be able to see where your materials are at every point in the supply chain, not just when they arrive at your door.
  • 02.
    Multi-source supplier relationships. Single-source dependency is the Achilles heel of JIT. Always have a qualified alternative supplier ready to activate.
  • 03.
    Predictive risk monitoring. Automated alerts for port congestion, weather disruption, and supplier performance anomalies give you the lead time to adjust before a delay becomes a crisis.
  • 04.
    Experienced logistics management. Technology enables JIT. Experienced project managers execute it. The human layer of judgement and relationship management is irreplaceable.

JIT is not dead. It has evolved. The businesses that master the new JIT — precision delivery backed by real-time intelligence and experienced management — will operate with a structural cost and agility advantage that their competitors will struggle to close.

Contractor Compliance in the UK: What Every Project Manager Must Know in 2025

Contractor compliance is one of the most underestimated risk areas in UK project management. A single non-compliant contractor on site can trigger HSE investigations, void your insurance, expose your organisation to unlimited liability, and halt an entire project. Yet the majority of UK businesses still manage contractor compliance through manual spreadsheets and periodic spot-checks that miss far more than they catch.

The Compliance Landscape in 2025

The regulatory environment for contractor management has become significantly more complex in recent years. The Building Safety Act 2022 introduced new duties for principal contractors and principal designers on higher-risk buildings. The Health and Safety at Work Act continues to place primary responsibility for site safety on the principal contractor. And the increasing use of subcontractor chains means that compliance failures several tiers down the supply chain can still land at the principal contractor's door.

The Five Documents Every Contractor Must Have Verified

  • 01.
    Public liability insurance. Minimum £5 million cover for most commercial projects, with the certificate verified as current and the scope confirmed as covering the specific work being undertaken.
  • 02.
    Employers liability insurance. Legally required for any contractor with employees. Minimum £5 million cover. Expiry dates must be tracked and renewals confirmed before the existing certificate lapses.
  • 03.
    CSCS cards and trade certifications. Every operative on site must hold a valid CSCS card appropriate to their role. Specialist trades require additional certifications — gas safe, NICEIC, IPAF, PASMA — that must be individually verified.
  • 04.
    Method statements and risk assessments. Site-specific RAMS must be reviewed and approved before any work begins. Generic RAMS that haven't been tailored to the specific site and task are not compliant.
  • 05.
    Tax compliance confirmation. Under the Construction Industry Scheme, principal contractors must verify the CIS registration status of all subcontractors before making payments. Failure to do so creates significant tax liability.

Why Manual Compliance Management Fails

The fundamental problem with spreadsheet-based compliance management is that it is static in a dynamic environment. Certificates expire. Insurance policies lapse. Operatives' cards run out. A compliance check conducted at contractor onboarding is out of date the moment any of these documents changes. Effective compliance management requires continuous monitoring — automated alerts when documents are approaching expiry, and instant visibility of any contractor whose compliance status has lapsed.

Automated Compliance: The Only Scalable Solution

For organisations managing more than a handful of contractors, automated compliance management is the only scalable solution. Troer's contractor compliance platform maintains a live compliance register for every contractor in your network — automatically flagging expiring documents, blocking non-compliant contractors from site access systems, and providing a complete audit trail that demonstrates due diligence in the event of an HSE investigation or insurance claim.

The cost of implementing automated compliance management is a fraction of the cost of a single compliance failure. One HSE improvement notice. One voided insurance claim. One project shutdown. Any of these outcomes costs more than a year of automated compliance monitoring. The question is not whether you can afford to automate — it's whether you can afford not to.

The Single Source of Truth: Why Your Supply Chain Needs One and How to Build It

Ask ten people in your organisation what your current stock levels are, what your top supplier's on-time delivery rate is, or how much you spent on procurement last quarter — and you will likely get ten different answers. This is the silo effect in action. And it is costing your business more than you realise in poor decisions, missed opportunities, and avoidable crises.

What Is a Single Source of Truth?

A Single Source of Truth (SSOT) is a centralised data environment where every stakeholder — from the warehouse operative to the chief executive — accesses the same real-time information. There is no version control problem. No conflicting spreadsheets. No decisions made on data that was accurate last Tuesday but has since changed. Every function in the business operates from the same live picture of operational reality.

The Business Cost of Data Silos

Data silos don't just create confusion — they create measurable financial damage. Procurement teams ordering stock that operations already has in a different warehouse. Finance reporting on costs that procurement has already renegotiated. Project managers making scheduling decisions based on delivery ETAs that logistics updated three hours ago but never communicated. Each of these failures is individually small. Collectively, across a complex supply chain operation, they compound into millions of pounds of avoidable waste annually.

The Four Layers of a Supply Chain SSOT

  • 01.
    Data integration layer. All data sources — ERP systems, logistics platforms, supplier portals, finance systems — must feed into a single integration layer that normalises and consolidates the data in real time.
  • 02.
    Master data management. Consistent definitions, naming conventions, and data standards across all systems ensure that the same product, supplier, or cost centre is described identically everywhere.
  • 03.
    Role-based visibility. Different stakeholders need different views of the same data. A warehouse manager needs operational detail. A finance director needs cost summaries. A chief executive needs strategic KPIs. The SSOT serves all of them from the same underlying data.
  • 04.
    Automated alerting. The SSOT should proactively push critical information to the right people at the right time — not wait for someone to log in and look for it.

Supply Chain Resilience in 2025: The Complete Guide for UK Businesses

Supply chain resilience has moved from boardroom buzzword to business-critical priority. The disruptions of the last five years — a global pandemic, the Suez Canal blockage, post-Brexit trade friction, energy price shocks, and escalating geopolitical instability — have demonstrated beyond any doubt that supply chains built purely for efficiency are dangerously fragile. In 2025, the question every UK business must answer is: how resilient is your supply chain, and what would it take to break it?

Defining True Supply Chain Resilience

Resilience is not the same as robustness. A robust supply chain resists disruption. A resilient supply chain absorbs disruption and recovers quickly. The distinction matters because in today's environment, disruption is inevitable. The goal is not to prevent every shock — it is to build the capability to absorb shocks and return to normal operations faster than your competitors.

The Resilience Maturity Model

Supply chain resilience exists on a maturity spectrum. At the lowest level, organisations are purely reactive — they respond to disruptions after they occur with no pre-planned response. At the highest level, organisations are fully adaptive — they continuously sense changes in their supply chain environment and adjust their operations dynamically before disruptions materialise. Most UK businesses sit somewhere in the middle, with pockets of good practice alongside significant vulnerabilities.

The Six Pillars of Supply Chain Resilience

  • 01.
    Supply chain mapping. You cannot protect what you cannot see. A complete map of your supply chain — including Tier-2 and Tier-3 suppliers — is the foundation of any resilience programme. Most organisations are shocked by how little visibility they have beyond their direct suppliers.
  • 02.
    Supplier diversification. Single-source dependency is the most common and most dangerous supply chain vulnerability. Every critical component or service should have at least one qualified alternative supplier ready to activate within 48 hours.
  • 03.
    Strategic inventory positioning. Not all inventory is equal. Identifying the critical components whose absence would halt operations and maintaining strategic buffers for those specific items — while running lean on everything else — balances resilience with efficiency.
  • 04.
    Real-time risk monitoring. Continuous monitoring of supplier performance, geopolitical risk, freight disruption, and weather events gives organisations the early warning they need to activate contingency plans before disruptions become crises.
  • 05.
    Documented contingency plans. When disruption hits, there is no time to design a response from scratch. Pre-documented contingency plans for the most likely disruption scenarios — key supplier failure, logistics disruption, demand spike — enable fast, coordinated responses.
  • 06.
    Cross-functional collaboration. Supply chain resilience cannot be owned by a single function. Procurement, operations, finance, and senior leadership must all be aligned on resilience priorities and empowered to act when disruption occurs.

Resilience for NHS and Public Sector Supply Chains

For NHS trusts and public sector organisations, supply chain resilience carries an additional dimension: the consequences of failure are measured not just in financial terms but in patient outcomes. A resilient NHS supply chain ensures that clinical teams always have the consumables, pharmaceuticals, and equipment they need — regardless of what is happening in the broader logistics environment. This requires a higher standard of resilience planning than most commercial organisations apply, with mandatory contingency stock levels, pre-approved alternative suppliers, and real-time visibility of supply status across all sites.

Building Resilience Without Sacrificing Efficiency

The most common objection to supply chain resilience investment is cost. Dual sourcing costs more than single sourcing. Safety stock ties up working capital. Resilience monitoring requires technology investment. These costs are real — but they must be weighed against the cost of disruption. A single major supply chain failure — a project shutdown, a clinical supply crisis, a customer-facing stockout — typically costs far more than a year of resilience investment. The organisations that have done this calculation honestly are investing in resilience. The ones that haven't are one disruption away from finding out why they should have.
